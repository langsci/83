% CHAPTER 5 PARTICLES
\chapter{Particles}\label{ch:particles}
This chapter covers particles\index[sub]{particles} in Southern Yauyos Quechua. In \SYQ{}, as in most other Quechuan languages, the class of particles can be sorted into seven sub-classes: interjections (\phono{\textexclamdown{}Alal\'aw!} `How cold!'); assenters and greetings (\phono{aw} `yes'); prepositions (\phono{asta} `until'); adverbs (\phono{ayvis} `sometimes'); coordinators (\phono{icha} `or'); negators (\phono{mana} `no, not'); and prenumerals (\phono{la}, \phono{las}, occurring with expressions of time). Interjections, assenters and greetings, prepositions, and adverbs are covered in \S~\ref{sec:interjections}--\ref{sec:adverbs}, respectively. Coordinators are discussed in \S~\ref{sec:coord} on coordination; negators, in \S~\ref{sec:negation} on negation; and prenumerals, in \S~\ref{ssec:timenum}  on time numerals and prenumerals.

\section{Interjections}\label{sec:interjections}\index[sub]{interjections}
All spontaneously attested indigenous exclamations share a common pattern: they begin with \phono{a} and end in \phono{w} or, less commonly, in \phono{k} or \phono{y} (a--h); with the exception of the final \phono{w}, they feature almost exclusively the alveolar and palatal consonants \phono{ch}, \phono{ll}, \phono{l}, \phono{n}, \phono{\~n}, \phono{t}, and \phono{y} (which accounts for the entire catalogue of \SYQ{} alveolars and palatals with the exception of voiceless fricatives \phono{s}, \phono{sh}, and retroflex \phono{tr}); they include no vowels except for \phono{a}; they consist, with few exceptions, of three or four syllables; and they bear stress on the final syllable. Syllable repetition is not uncommon. Non-exclamatory interjections do not follow this pattern (i), (j). Curse words are freely borrowed from Spanish (k--m). (1--7) give a few examples.

\section{Assenters and greetings}
The list of assenters\index[sub]{assenters} includes three members: \phono{ar\'i}, \phono{aw}, and \phono{alal} (1), (2). The first and second are used in all dialects; the third, only in \CH{}. \phono{ar\'i} often carries the emphatic enclitic \phono{-y\'a} (3). \phono{aw} is used to check for agreement from interlocutors and in the formulation of tag questions (4), (5). Speakers of \SYQ{} make heavy use of the borrowed Spanish greetings\index[sub]{greetings}, \phono{buynus diyas} `good day', \phono{buynas tardis} `good afternoon' and \phono{buynas nuchis} `good evening', `good night' (6). \phono{\textexclamdown{}Rimallasayki!} `I greet you!' is the most common of the greetings indigenous to \SYQ{}. \phono{\textexclamdown{}Saludallasayki!} is also used.

\section{Prepositions}
\SYQ{} makes use of some prepositions\index[sub]{prepositions} borrowed from Spanish. The preposition most frequently employed is \phono{asta} (`up to', `until', `even', from Spanish `\spanish{hasta}' `up to', `until') (1). \phono{asta} is usually employed redundantly, in combination with the indigenous case suffix \phono{-kama}, apparently with the same semantics (\phono{asta aka-kama} `until until here').

\section{Adverbs}\label{sec:adverbs}
The class of adverbs\index[sub]{adverbs} native to \SYQ{} is rather small (1--3). Verbal modification in \SYQ{}, as in other Quechuan languages, is accomplished primarily by derivatives and enclitics (\phono{-pa} `repeatedly', \phono{-\~na} `already'). \SYQ{} makes heavy use of the adoped/adapted Spanish adverbs \phono{apuraw} `quick', \phono{pasaypaq} `completely,' \phono{siympri} `always' and \phono{ayvis} `sometimes' (4--7). Additionally, adverbs can sometimes be derived from adjectives with the suffixation of \phono{-lla} (8), (9); and adjectives may sometimes occur adverbally, in which case they are usually inflected with \phono{-ta} (10--12). Some nouns referring to time may occur adverbally without inflection (13), (14), others are inflected with \phono{-ta} (see \S~\ref{ssec:timenouns}) (15).

\section{Particles covered elsewhere}
Coordinators are discussed in \S~\ref{sec:coord} on coordination; negators, in \S~\ref{sec:negation} on negation; and prenumerals, in Sub\S~\ref{ssec:timenum} on time numerals and prenumerals.
